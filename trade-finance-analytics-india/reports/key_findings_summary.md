# Key Findings Summary

> One-page summary of the primary findings from the Trade Finance Analytics project.
> Intended audience: business stakeholders, policy readers, and recruiters reviewing the project.

## Finding 1 — 2024 Marks a Structural Inflection Point

Factoring volume in 2024 (38.20K M EUR) accounted for **41.02%** of the entire 7-year cumulative total. This is not a statistical anomaly — it reflects the compounding effect of RBI's TReDS (Trade Receivables Discounting System) reforms, increased MSME formalisation, and post-pandemic credit appetite. The market is not linear; it is accelerating.

**Implication:** Financial institutions entering the factoring market today are entering at the beginning of an exponential phase, not the tail end.

---

## Finding 2 — COVID-19 Was a Temporary, Not Structural, Disruption

The 2020 dip (-30% factoring volume, negative export growth) was sharp but short-lived. By 2021, both metrics had recovered, with factoring growth exceeding 130% YoY. This resilience pattern — sharp shock, rapid recovery — mirrors the behaviour of mature financial markets, suggesting India's trade finance ecosystem has developed structural robustness.

**Implication:** Investors and lenders treating the 2020 data as representative of long-term risk are likely mis-pricing that risk.

---

## Finding 3 — Forfaiting Volume and Export Performance Are Positively Correlated

Scatter analysis of forfaiting volume (X-axis) vs export value (Y-axis) for 2015–2024 shows a near-monotonic positive relationship. As forfaiting uptake grew from ~$5B to ~$15B, exports grew from $265B to ~$480B. While correlation does not prove causation, the consistency of the relationship across 10 years — including the COVID disruption period — is statistically meaningful.

**Implication:** Policies that increase forfaiting accessibility for MSME exporters may have a measurable positive effect on total export performance.

---

## Finding 4 — An 18-Month Adoption Lag Reveals an Awareness Problem

In 2020–2021, export performance recovered faster than forfaiting volume adoption. This 18-month lag suggests that the constraint on forfaiting uptake is not economic demand (exports were growing) but rather **awareness and access** — MSME exporters either don't know forfaiting exists or cannot easily access it through their banking relationships.

**Implication:** High-ROI policy interventions include bank branch training, EXIM Bank awareness campaigns, and simplified forfaiting product structures — not just subsidised rates.

---

## Limitations

- Forfaiting volume data is estimated; informal/unreported transactions are excluded
- EUR-denominated factoring data is nominal (not inflation-adjusted)
- Correlation analysis does not establish causality between forfaiting and exports
- Sectoral breakdown of factoring beneficiaries is not available in public FIMIS data
